Introduction

Moving money from a workplace retirement plan — or from one IRA to another — sounds straightforward. In reality, it's one of the most consequential financial decisions you'll make. Done right, a rollover can protect your savings from unnecessary taxes, preserve your growth potential, and set you up for reliable retirement income.

Done wrong, a single mistake can cost thousands of dollars in taxes and penalties, lock your money into a high-fee account, or disrupt your entire retirement plan.

Who This Guide Is For

Adults age 45+ who have a 401(k), 403(b), TSP, or traditional IRA and are considering moving those funds — whether due to a job change, retirement, or a routine account review.

This guide walks you through the five most common mistakes I see when people move retirement accounts — and what to do instead.

1
Mistake #1
Taking a Cash Distribution Instead of a Direct Rollover

When you leave an employer, you may be offered a check for your 401(k) balance. Accepting it is almost always the most expensive way to move your money.

What happens: Your employer must withhold 20% for federal taxes automatically. Under age 59½, you'll also owe a 10% early withdrawal penalty — plus state income tax.
Example

A $120,000 account triggers $24,000 in automatic withholding. If you're in the 22% bracket and under 59½, total taxes and penalties could reduce your balance by $38,400 or more.

What to do instead: Request a direct rollover — the funds transfer directly to your new IRA or plan. No taxes withheld, no penalties. If you receive a check made out to you, you have 60 days to deposit the full amount (including the withheld 20%) into a qualified account to avoid taxes.
2
Mistake #2
Rolling Over Without Reviewing Fees and Investment Options

Many people roll their 401(k) into the first account they're offered without comparing fees or investment options. An expense ratio difference of just 0.5%–1% per year can cost tens of thousands of dollars in lost growth over 15 years on a $200,000 account.

Watch out for:
  • High fund expense ratios buried in the fine print
  • Annual maintenance or custodian fees
  • Surrender charges and lock-up periods
  • Limited investment menus that don't fit your strategy
What to do instead: Request a full fee disclosure before completing the rollover. Compare at least two or three options, and make sure the investment lineup aligns with your timeline, risk tolerance, and income goals.

3
Mistake #3
Missing a Roth Conversion Opportunity

Most rollovers move pre-tax money into a traditional IRA — keeping it tax-deferred. That's often correct. But for some people at certain income levels, a rollover is the best opportunity they'll have to convert some or all of those funds to a Roth IRA, which grows and distributes tax-free.

Timing matters: The years between leaving a job and starting Social Security or Required Minimum Distributions often create a window of lower taxable income — making this the ideal time to convert at a lower rate.

Consider a Roth conversion if:

  • You're in a lower tax bracket than you expect to be in retirement
  • You have cash outside your retirement accounts to pay the conversion tax
  • You want to reduce future Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs)
  • You want to leave tax-free inheritance to beneficiaries
What to do instead: Before completing a rollover, discuss whether a full or partial Roth conversion makes sense. Under current tax law, conversions cannot be undone — so this decision must be made before the rollover is finalized.
4
Mistake #4
Making the Move Without a Retirement Income Plan

A rollover moves your money. It doesn't tell your money what to do next. One of the most common oversights is completing a rollover without a clear plan for how and when you'll draw income from it in retirement.

Common coordination mistakes:
  • Withdrawing from tax-deferred accounts before claiming Social Security, raising taxable income unnecessarily
  • Not planning for Required Minimum Distributions starting at age 73
  • Ignoring how IRA distributions affect Medicare Part B & D premiums (IRMAA)
  • Leaving all assets in growth investments past the point where income reliability matters
What to do instead: Use the rollover as a catalyst to build or revisit your retirement income plan. Where will monthly income come from? In what order will you draw from different accounts? Answering these questions before you move the money helps you place it where it will work best.
5
Mistake #5
Deciding Without Reviewing All of Your Options

When you leave a job, you typically have four options: leave the money in your former plan, roll it to an IRA, roll it to a new employer's plan, or take a cash distribution. Most people know to avoid the cash-out — but fewer genuinely compare the first three.

Leave it in the former plan — may make sense if the plan has excellent low-cost institutional funds or strong creditor protection. You lose the Rule of 55 benefit once you've left that employer.

Roll to an IRA — typically provides broader investment flexibility, direct control, Roth conversion options, and simpler estate planning.

Roll to a new employer's plan — can be useful to preserve loan access, or to delay RMDs past age 73 if you're still employed.

What to do instead: Request a full fee disclosure and plan document from your former employer. Compare it against the IRA or plan you're considering. Some older plans include annuity income options or stable-value funds worth preserving — don't roll out of something good simply because it's the default move.

Putting It All Together

A retirement account rollover is more than a paperwork transaction. It's a financial decision that affects your taxes, your investment growth, and your retirement income for decades. The five mistakes in this guide are all avoidable — but only if you know to look for them before you act.

The most important thing you can do is slow down. You are not required to move quickly. You have time to compare options, consult a professional, and choose the path that's right for your situation — not just the easiest one or the one your HR department recommends.

The years approaching retirement are the most impactful financial planning years of your life. The decisions you make with your retirement accounts in this window — where you hold the money, how it's positioned, whether you convert any of it, and how you coordinate withdrawals — will shape your income and tax situation for the rest of your life.

Working with an independent financial professional who specializes in retirement planning can make this process significantly simpler. The right advisor can model different scenarios side-by-side, explain the tax implications clearly, and help you choose the path that aligns with your goals — without pushing you toward any single product or company.
